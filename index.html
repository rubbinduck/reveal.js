Quick Rundown of Definitions and Notation
We denote by $\mS^d$ the vector space of symmetric $d\times d$ matrices, by $\mS^d_{\geq 0}$ (resp. $\mS^d_{>0}$) the cone of positive semidefinite (resp. definite) matrices and by $\IR^d_{\geq 0}$ (resp. $\IZ^d_{\geq 0}$) the set of componentwise non-negative (integral) vectors.
Let \[ \cop^d = \left\{ Q\in\mS^d: x^\intercal Qx \geq 0 \text{ for all } x\in\IR^d_{\geq 0} \right\} \] denote the cone of copositive matrices of order $d$ and \[ \cp^d = \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{n}x_ix_i^\intercal: n\in\mathbb{N}, x_i\in\IR^d_{\geq 0} \right\} \] the cone of completely positive matrices of order $d$ .
\[ \minC Q = \min_{x\in\IZ^d_{\geq 0}}Q[x] \] \[ \MinC Q = \bigg\{ x\in\IZ^d_{\geq 0}: Q[x] = \minC Q \bigg\} \] For matrices $Q\in\oint\cop^d$ we have \[ \minC Q > 0 \text{ and } |\MinC Q| < \infty. \]
A matrix $Q\in\oint\cop^d$ is called perfect copositive , if the set \[ \mV(Q) = \bigg\{ xx^\intercal: x\in\MinC Q \bigg\} \] has a non-empty interior in $\mS^d$.
Connecting the Classics with the Newfangled
Theorem:
Every classically perfect matrix is artihmetically equivalent to a classically perfect matrix, which is also perfect copositive.
Classifying all positive semidefinite perfect copositive matrices
Theorem:
Let $P$ be positive semidefinite and perfect copositive with $\ork P=k \geq 2$. Then there exists a $U\in\text{GL}_n(\IZ)$ and a perfect copositive $\widetilde{P}\in\mS^k_{>0}$, such that \[ U^\intercal PU = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \widetilde{P} \end{pmatrix}. \]
"Difficult" part is showing $\widetilde{P}$ to be perfect copositive.
For positive semidefinite (in fact all non-full rank) matrices $P$ we have \[ \MinC P = (\{ x_1, \dots x_N \} + \ker P)\cap\IZ^n_{\geq 0}. \] If the $x_i$ are chosen such that for $i\neq j$ \[ x_i + \ker P \cap x_j + \ker P = \emptyset, \] we say that they generate $\MinC P$.
Corollary:
If $\ork P=k$, then $\MinC P$ has $N \geq \binom{k+1}{2}$ generators $x_1, \dots, x_N$.
Easy to prove using the "explicit" construction of $U$ in the theorem.
